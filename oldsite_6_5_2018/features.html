Badminton: governing body plans 'exciting' changes to scoring.

Badminton will trial a new scoring system from August with a view to using it at the 2016 Olympics.

Under the new rules, matches would be best of five games rather than three, and games would be played to 11 points instead of 21.

Badminton World Federation president Poul-Erik Hoyer said the length of games under the current format "compromised the level of excitement".

But many players oppose the changes, saying they will lead to confusion.

Five-time world champion Lin Dan has voiced concern over the new system, which will initially be tried out at selected tournaments.

"I don't like it," he said. "I don't think making changes like this is good for the game."
The new scoring system would need to be implemented across the board before May 2015 if it is to be used at the 2016 Olympics in Rio de Janeiro, Brazil.

'Pick up a racquet ladies!'
24th February 2009
By Ben Ellery

MORE women in Basingstoke should be smashing shuttlecocks according to local badminton clubs.The number of female players in the borough has fallen and a drive has been launched to increase the quota.

Ladies who have never played the game, or who used to play, are being urged to pick up their racquets for a series of sessions at Everest Community College, in Sherborne Road, Sherborne St John, Basingstoke.

Great Britain has produced a good crop of Badminton players, with the likes of Gail Emms and Nathan Robertson gaining silver medals at the 2004 Olympics, and it is hoped more stars can be found in Basingstoke.

Chairman of the Basingstoke and District Badminton League, Ruth Cairns, said: “We have 15 clubs in the area, with more than 300 games played a season and all the clubs are short of ladies.

“Women tend to play badminton at school but stop when they leave, whereas men carry on. It’s a fantastic sport, and more people play it recreationally than football in this country, we would just like to see more competitive players.

Louise O’Neill, of Cavalier Road, Old Basing, was borough along to a session by a friend. She said: “I used to play badminton at school but stopped. It’s a very fast game and is great for exercise, I think it’s brilliant more women are being encouraged to play.”
